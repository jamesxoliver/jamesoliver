# Record

**Record:** An encoding stabilized outside its source so that it remains recoverable after the source event has ended.

A spoken sentence ordinarily disappears as its pressure pattern disperses. If the sentence is written or captured by a recording system, its structure is converted into a configuration that remains after the speaker stops acting. The original event does not survive. What survives is an arrangement capable of producing a sufficiently similar reconstruction.

A record can persist in two ways. Each copy can resist destruction, or copies can be produced and distributed faster than they disappear. A stone inscription relies primarily on the durability of one instance. A digital file relies primarily on inexpensive, high-fidelity replication. A book combines moderate durability with repeatability and distribution.

For a passive record, durability depends on the barrier protecting its encoded states from unintended change. A state that ordinary perturbations can easily alter decays quickly, while a state separated from alternatives by a larger energy barrier remains recoverable longer. Raising that barrier generally makes the state harder to create, alter, or erase, although particular mechanisms can change the cost of crossing it.

Information survives through the continued recoverability of a physical difference, whether one instance endures or a lineage of copies does.
