# Lexical Constraint

**Lexical constraint:** A word constrains the possibility space through which thought searches.

## Ingredients

- [Word](../../definitions/source/word.md)
- [Context](../../definitions/source/context.md)
- [Information](../../definitions/source/information.md)
- [Possibility Space](../../definitions/source/possibility-space.md)
- [Representation](../../definitions/source/representation.md)
- [Error](../../definitions/source/error.md)

## Derivation

A word draws a symbolic boundary around a region of possible meaning. Encountering the word therefore supplies information: interpretations outside that boundary become less available, while interpretations inside it become more available. Thought does not begin from the whole territory. It begins inside the region activated by the representation.

The word *Georgia* can activate at least two geographic maps. If the surrounding context is insufficient, a listener may search the country when the speaker means the American state. Every inference that follows may be internally coherent while referring to the wrong region. The reasoning did not first fail at its conclusion. The word placed it in the wrong possibility space before the reasoning began.

The same mechanism produces several failures. A word can activate the wrong referent. It can divide phenomena that share a causal mechanism, unite phenomena generated by different mechanisms, or make a process appear to be an independent object. In each case, the inherited boundary constrains which relationships can be seen and which questions can be asked.

Language models make the operation visible. Before a prompt, many outputs are possible. Each word conditions the distribution of subsequent outputs:

\[
P(\text{output}) \rightarrow P(\text{output}\mid\text{prompt})
\]

Natural-language constraints are usually probabilistic rather than absolute. A word raises some continuations and suppresses others without necessarily making either impossible. Context can correct the initial region, but it must supply the information the word did not.

The general chain is:

\[
\text{Word}
\rightarrow
\text{Semantic boundary}
\rightarrow
\text{Search region}
\rightarrow
\text{Available relationships}
\rightarrow
\text{Conclusion}
\]

Words are necessary compression. The failure is not that they impose boundaries, because thought without boundaries cannot begin. The failure occurs when a boundary in the representation is mistaken for a boundary in reality.

## Test

Hold the underlying task constant and replace the word used to frame it. If the replacement changes which referents, relationships, or solutions become available to a mind or model, the word was functioning as a constraint on the search space. The claim fails if lexical framing never changes the region through which otherwise equivalent reasoning proceeds.
